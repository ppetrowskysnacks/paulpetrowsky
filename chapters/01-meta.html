01 Architecting Meta's Support

Designing the system that designs the flows.

Five years inside Central Integrity & Support — rebuilding the layer underneath every help, recovery, and report experience across Facebook, Instagram, and Meta's business surfaces.

Role
Principal Product Designer
Team
Central Integrity & Support
Years
2021 — Present
Surfaces
Facebook · Instagram · Business
01 Challenge

The wall behind the report button.

Across Meta's surfaces, billions of people hit the same wall when something goes wrong. They report a post. They appeal a decision. They try to recover an account. Their request goes into a queue. The flow tells them very little. They never quite know if they were heard.

That wall wasn't intentional. It was the accumulated result of each team solving support in their own corner, with their own primitives, on their own timeline. The patchwork read — accurately — like the company wasn't listening.

I joined Meta's Central Integrity & Support team in 2021 to design support flows. About six months in, the real problem came into focus: better flows weren't going to fix this. We needed something underneath the flows.

02 Approach

Stop drawing flows. Start designing the system that draws them.

The reframe was this: pull out a small set of reusable primitives. Wire them to a shared taxonomy of problems, audiences, and recovery paths. Connect the taxonomy to the model that decides what to surface next. Once that exists, teams across the company can ship support experiences without inventing a new vocabulary every time.

The work spans research, product strategy, design systems, information architecture, and — increasingly — the prompts and rails for AI-assisted recovery. Six product surfaces. Three product organizations. One shared layer underneath.

These days I'm not designing flows — I'm designing the system that designs the flows. Paul, year four
Three moves

  1. Map the territory

    Audit every existing support experience across IG, Facebook, and Business. Tag the patterns. Surface where teams diverged silently.

  2. Build the primitives

    A small composable kit — status, context, action, recovery, escalation — that any team can stitch into a flow without re-litigating the basics.

  3. Wire the brain

    Connect primitives to a shared taxonomy + AI-assisted recovery so the system decides what to show, not just where to put it.

03 Outcome

From a wall into a system.

The first public-facing wave shipped in December 2025 — people can now see what's happening with their reports, follow up, and recover the account, post, or ad without getting routed to a human queue first.

Six surfaces are live on the shared layer. The patterns are documented, the taxonomy is owned by partners across three orgs, and AI-assisted recovery is rolling out one slice at a time. The system isn't done — it's the kind of work that never really is — but for the first time, it's a system.
